The throttle bar never moved. A hover throttle only balances gravity while the drone is at rest; it has no idea the aircraft is low, because nothing in it ever looks at the gap. Drag bleeds off the fall, so the drone just parks below the setpoint and forgets it was ever supposed to be at 10 m. That is a fixed command — and it is exactly what altitude hold is not.

The idea

A feedback controller acts on the error, not on a number it memorised

Every control cycle — for a drone, typically 50 to 400 times a second — the controller does one small piece of arithmetic:

error  e = setpoint − measurement

Then it commands the actuator (the motors) in proportion to that error, in the direction that shrinks it: low → push thrust up, high → ease thrust off. Because the command is made of the error, the loop closes itself: as the measured altitude climbs back toward the setpoint, the error shrinks, so the correction shrinks, and the drone settles right on the line. Nobody stored a magic throttle value — the error is the input.

Retrieval · no peeking at the widget

Bring it back from memory

In a drone's altitude hold, what does the controller actually act on each control cycle, and why does that make the aircraft self-correct rather than just holding a fixed motor command?

Each cycle it computes the error, e = setpoint − measured altitude (desired minus actual). It doesn't send a stored throttle; it drives the motors from that error, so any nonzero error produces a correction — low pushes thrust up, high eases it off. As the drone nears the setpoint the error shrinks, so the correction shrinks too, and it settles right on the setpoint: the loop closes. A fixed throttle can't self-correct because it never looks at the error.

Same loop, different machine

A home thermostat holds a room at 21°C as the weather outside changes. In feedback terms, what two quantities is it comparing, and what makes the furnace switch on when a cold front hits?

It compares the setpoint (21°C) with the measured room temperature: error = 21 − actual. When a cold front drops the room below 21, the error goes positive, and that nonzero error is what switches the furnace on; as the room warms back to 21 the error closes to zero and it switches off. Exactly the drone's loop: act on setpoint minus measurement, correct until the error disappears.
