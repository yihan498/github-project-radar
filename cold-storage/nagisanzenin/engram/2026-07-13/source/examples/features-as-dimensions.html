Q2. Why does adding a feature add a dimension? What breaks if you just fold the new number into an existing axis?

My answer is committed — reveal
Independence. A new measurement can vary while every old one stays fixed, so it needs a direction of its own. Fold it into an old axis and two unrelated quantities are forced to move together — faces differing only in the new feature collapse onto the same point.

The skeleton — compare against yours
A feature is one measured dimension. Each feature is an axis, and an example's values place it as a single point. Moving one slider is moving along one axis — the face and the vector are the same object. Deleting axes projects the space down, and points that differed only on the deleted axes become indistinguishable. Which is why feature choice decides what a model can ever separate.

What you just used, by the rules it obeys

Engram generates these under a binding seven-clause contract, because a beautiful page you read passively is a fluency trap — it feels like learning and isn't. Every rule here traces to a measured effect:

  • Nothing unlocks without a commitment. Guided discovery beats free exploration (d = +0.30 vs −0.38 for unguided).
  • The model is guided, never a sandbox. Scaffolded simulations beat the identical unscaffolded ones (g+ = 0.60).
  • A worked drive comes first for novices, then free manipulation — expertise reversal is real (+0.505 novices with assistance, −0.428 for the knowledgeable).
  • Zero decoration. Seductive details reliably depress learning. Nothing on this page is here to look nice.
  • It ends with a blank page, because retrieval is the treatment, not the measurement.
